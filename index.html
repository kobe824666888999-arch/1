The Real Problems

6 reasons your Chinese sounds foreign — even when it's "correct."

01 · Tones

Textbooks teach tones. Nobody teaches tone feel.

You know 1st, 2nd, 3rd, 4th tones. But real speech is fast, blended, and full of neutral tones and tone sandhi your course never mentioned. You sound robotic even when you're technically right.

"My tones are correct but native speakers still sound like a different language."
02 · Vocabulary

You learned words. Nobody uses them.

HSK vocabulary is chosen for tests, not conversation. Real Chinese uses slang, particles, filler words, and scene-specific phrases that no textbook includes. Your vocabulary is technically correct and socially invisible.

"I said 非常感谢 and they looked at me like I was reading from a script."
03 · Rhythm

Chinese has a spoken rhythm. Textbooks don't teach it.

Native Chinese speech has sentence-level melody, hesitation sounds (那个, 就是说), filler particles (嘛, 啊, 吧), and pacing patterns. Without these, every sentence you say sounds like a translation.

"I understand everything when I prepare — but real conversations overwhelm me in seconds."
04 · Culture

Technically correct but socially wrong

Chinese communication is as much about what's NOT said. Face (面子), indirect refusals, compliment etiquette, office hierarchy rules — these social codes aren't in any textbook, but violating them marks you immediately.

"I was grammatically perfect and accidentally rude at a business dinner."
05 · Scenarios

You practiced 1 scenario. Life has 100.

Apps teach airport, restaurant, hotel. Real life is: WeChat group chats, supplier negotiation, meeting a partner's parents, bartering at a market, complaining to a manager. Your Chinese fails the moment you leave the textbook scene.

"I can order food fine. Everything else — I freeze immediately."
06 · Confidence

The shame loop kills more learners than grammar

You say something "correct." Natives react confused or smile politely and switch to English. That embarrassment sticks. You hesitate more. Your brain starts translating again. The loop gets worse. This isn't your fault — it's a teaching failure.

"I knew the words. I just couldn't make them come out natural."

My Chinese is "correct" but people always switch to English with me. Why?

Because "correct" signals effort, not fluency.
